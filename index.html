San Francisco Crime Data

A data-driven exploration of crime patterns (2003–2025)

This project explores patterns in San Francisco crime data over the past 20 years. Through interactive visualizations, we uncover trends across time, location, and crime type.

Our analysis is based on crime data from 2003 to 2025, drawn from two datasets published by the San Francisco Police Department through DataSF. These datasets were merged into one, requiring several modifications.

Some crime categories were originally classified differently, and variables such as date formats needed to be standardized. After cleaning and preparing the data, we selected nine crime categories that were both relevant and consistent across datasets.

Through our analysis, we developed a strong understanding of the dataset and identified several interesting patterns. In particular, drug-related crimes stood out due to notable trends including a strong geographic concentration in the Tenderloin district and distinct changes over time..

This report explores these findings in more depth, with a focus on uncovering meaningful insights from the data.

Drugs in San Francisco

When exploring the data, one crime category quickly stood out to us. We had heard about the drug crisis in San Francisco, especially related to fentanyl, and wanted to explore this more closely. This issue is often linked to challenges the city has been facing, including homelessness and public health concerns. But we wanted to understand it from the data: is this happening all over the city, or only in specific areas? On the following bar plot, we look at drug incidents by district relative to the citywide average. The value shown is a relative ratio: the share of incidents classified as drug-related within a district divided by the citywide share of drug-related incidents. A value of 1 means that a district matches the city average, while values above 1 indicate overrepresentation. Tenderloin reaches roughly 4.4, meaning that drug-related incidents make up a much larger share of recorded incidents there than they do across San Francisco overall. Most other districts are close to 1 or below, suggesting that drug-related incidents are not equally distributed across the city.

The results are quite striking. The Tenderloin district clearly stands out, with more than four times the city average, far higher than any other district. What is also interesting is that most other districts are below the city average. Only a few, like Mission and Southern, are slightly above average, but still nowhere near the level of the Tenderloin. This shows that drug-related incidents are not evenly spread across the city, but are instead highly concentrated in specific areas. Local reporting on San Francisco’s fentanyl crisis suggests that Tenderloin has remained one of the city’s most visible drug-related hotspots. At the same time, the pattern may reflect not only underlying activity, but also differences in policing, enforcement, and reporting practices. (Source: SF Standard)

Heatmap

The bar chart shows that drug-related incidents are not evenly distributed across San Francisco, and that the Tenderloin stands out as a clear outlier. But we also see higher values in a couple of other districts. The figure alone does not show how these districts relate to each other geographically. To understand whether this concentration is in a isolated space or part of a broader spatial pattern, we now turn to the map.

From the heatmap, we can see that the Tenderloin is actually a very small area compared to the size of the city. This means that many visitors, or even residents, might not notice this pattern in their daily lives unless they are in that specific area. Darker shades on the map represent a higher drug ratio, meaning that drug-related incidents make up a larger share of recorded incidents in that district than they do citywide. The color scale is capped at 2 to make it easier to compare most districts, since the Tenderloin is such an extreme outlier. Even with this cap, it clearly stands out. Nearby districts such as Mission and Southern are somewhat elevated as well, pointing to a broader central concentration and suggesting that this is not completely isolated to Tenderloin. This interpretation is supported by local reporting from ABC7, which described 16th and Mission as a visible hotspot for both drug activity and illegal street vending, and reported that police had observed part of the drug scene shifting into Mission. However, Tenderloin remains by far the most concentrated hotspot according to the data. (Source: ABC7 News)

Trends over time

It is interesting to observe that drug incidents seem to cluster around the Tenderloin area. To understand this a bit better, we wanted to look at how things have changed over time. Has Tenderloin always been the main hotspot for drug offenses or is this something that developed more recently? To explore this, we plotted the number of drug offenses per year and broke them down by police district. We also included the city median as a reference point, to make comparison easier. Since Tenderloin stands out as a high-incident area, we ranked the districts based on how close they were to Tenderloin. You can both select specific districts, or choose to look at either the two or four districts closest to Tenderloin and compare them.

Looking at this plot, it is clear that Tenderloin has the highest number of drug offenses and that has been consistent throughout the years.

There is a clear spike around 2008–2009, and interestingly, this spike only appears in Tenderloin. Looking into it, we found that around 2010 there were changes in law enforcement priorities that could help explain this pattern.

In 2010, California changed the law so that possession of small amounts of marijuana became an infraction rather than a misdemeanor. This meant that these cases were no longer handled or reported in the same way as before. After this change, drug arrests in San Francisco and the Bay Area dropped significantly, by around 75% over the following years. This is clearly reflected in the data, where we see a sharp decline after 2009. (Source: DrugAbuse.com)

Another interesting trend is the drop in Tenderloin around 2017, where the number of drug offenses was almost the same as the city median. Digging a bit deeper, it seems that around 2018–2019, police in San Francisco were focusing more on investigating larger drug networks, which may have affected the resources available for street-level arrests. This could help explain the dip—fewer arrests don’t necessarily mean less activity, but rather a shift in focus. (Source: CBS News)
